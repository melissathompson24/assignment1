About Section 508 Standards, Universal Design, and Accessibility Approaches

Published on January 15, 2014.

Over the past few months at work I have been asked about Section 508 and what it really means within the context of the work we do. I currently work for a non-profit, human-services institute, housed within a university. We are 99% grant-funded, and many of our projects are federally funded. This is why Section 508 has started creeping into our conversations.

At first, I was just as unclear about what this deceptively innocuously sounding law means and its implications to us. But since I figured that in the case of federal government dollars, ignorance is not bliss, I had better begin studying-up on this dense law and its implications to us.

Although my exploration into this law is only at the beginning, here are my findings so far…

At the heart of Section 508 is the expectation that any electronic and information technology that is developed using federal dollars or for federal use is also developed in a way that makes it accessible to those with disabilities. You can read the official definition on Section 508 website, but be warned—as expected on a federal site, it is long and convoluted.

As the Section 508 website goes on to explain, this law covers the following systems developed under, for, or when used by, the federal government:

  • software applications and operating systems
  • web-based information or applications
  • telecommunication products
  • video and multimedia products
  • self-contained, closed products (e.g., information kiosks, calculators, and fax machines)
  • desktop and portable computers

As I became more familiar with this law, I also became aware of the complementary Universal Design movement that is become more widespread in the field of instructional design. Basically Universal Design centers around the idea to “[make] things safer, easier and more convenient for everyone.”

Applying Universal Design principles to designing systems and products would mean that they would be designed to allow anyone to access the information in a variety of ways. The development of a video can be used as an example of this principle: Not only would the video be developed to be watched, but captioning would also be included as an option, and possibly a script made available as well. This would allow a person to access the video in the way that make it easiest for that person to process the information, whether it be through watching, listening, or reading the information.

Of course there are many other considerations when designing accessible systems and products. The National Center on Disability and Access to Education summarizes some general global principles nicely on their website. They list the following practices:

  • the use of alt text for any graphics
  • content that is clearly-written and structured-well
  • use of headers in data tables
  • ensuring links make sense out of context
  • caption and provide transcripts for media
  • ensure users can complete and submit all forms
  • don’t rely on color to convey meaning
  • design to standards
  • ensure accessibility on non-HTML content

I realize this is truly a shallow-dive into the world of Section 508, Universal Design, and accessibility, but as I progress on my understanding of this world and its practices, tools, and resources, I am finding that this theme is becoming more widespread across industries, whether it’s government contracting and funding, instructional design and education, or home design. The ubiquitous expectation and application of accessibility has made this approach to design in all of our work, no matter the audience or funder, a common practice that cannot be ignored.
